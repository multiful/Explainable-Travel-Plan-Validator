# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Korean travelers planning domestic trips — solo, couples, friend groups, families, and elderly travelers with accessibility needs. They are actively mid-planning: they have a destination and rough ideas, but haven't confirmed whether the plan is physically achievable. They use Qtrip in a relaxed pre-trip context, typically at home on desktop or on a phone during a commute, not under time pressure but with genuine curiosity about whether their itinerary will actually work.

## Product Purpose

Qtrip validates whether a travel plan is feasible: does the route make sense geographically, do the operating hours hold up, is there enough time between each stop? It translates gut-feeling itineraries into data-backed confidence. Success means a user leaves with either reassurance that their plan works, or concrete, actionable fixes to make it work.

## Brand Personality

Honest friend. Direct, warm, slightly witty — like a well-traveled local who knows the places and will tell you the truth without making you feel bad about a flawed plan. Not corporate, not overly cheerful. The voice earns trust by being specific and honest, not by being enthusiastic.

Three words: **직접적, 따뜻한, 솔직한** (Direct, Warm, Honest)

## Anti-references

- **Corporate travel portals** (Expedia, Booking.com): dense, transactional, ad-heavy, zero personality.
- **Generic SaaS dashboards**: navy sidebar, metric card grids, identical table rows. Information without voice.
- **Overly playful travel apps**: confetti, mascots, cartoon UI. Loses credibility. Not appropriate for a validation tool used by adults.
- **Heavy data-viz tools** (Tableau, Google Analytics): cold and analytical. No warmth, no conversational quality.

## Design Principles

1. **Tell the truth with warmth.** Validation failures should feel like advice from a friend, not a system error. Always pair a problem with a path forward.
2. **Specificity over enthusiasm.** A score of 72 is more trustworthy than "Great plan!" Concrete numbers and named places build confidence.
3. **Respect the plan.** Users invested time in their itinerary. The UI should treat the plan as something worth saving, showing, and improving — not as throwaway input.
4. **Signal without noise.** The result is complex (hard fails, warnings, scores, route comparison, map). Hierarchy and progressive disclosure keep it from overwhelming.
5. **Earn each interaction.** No dark patterns, no push for sign-up, no artificial urgency. The product should feel calm and confident.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target: WCAG AA minimum, push toward AAA where practical (44px touch targets, enhanced contrast, descriptive ARIA labels). The product includes barrier-free travel features for elderly and mobility-impaired users — accessibility is part of the product promise, not just a compliance checkbox.
